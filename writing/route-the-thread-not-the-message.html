Cut LLM costs by routing the thread, not the message

June 2026 | LLM Systems

If you run a chatbot on a single large model, you are paying frontier prices to answer "what's the capital of Norway." If you run it on a single small model, the hard questions come back wrong. Neither is the deal you want.

The obvious fix is to stop sending every message to the same model. Send the easy ones to something cheap and fast, and save the expensive model for the questions that actually need it. On paper this is simple. In a real multi-turn conversation it is not, and the reason it breaks is interesting enough to be worth walking through.

What "the right model" actually means

Two things decide which model a message needs, and they are not the same thing.

The first is topic. A coding question and a travel question are best served by different models, even at the same level of difficulty. Some models are simply better at code; others are cheaper and perfectly good at planning a trip or answering general knowledge.

The second is complexity. "What time zone is Tokyo in" and "rewrite this booking flow to handle partial refunds and retries" are both travel-ish, but one is a lookup and the other needs real reasoning. You would not spend the same model on both.

In practice topic carries a lot of the complexity signal for free. Coding threads skew hard, casual general-knowledge threads skew easy, and you can encode that prior directly in how you map topics to models: point the coding bucket at your strong model and the general bucket at your cheap one. That is the lever this post leans on. Treating complexity as its own separate score is a clean extension, and it has the exact same shape as topic routing, but you can get most of the savings just by routing well on topic.

So the goal is concrete: look at each turn, decide what it is about and roughly how hard it is, and send it to the cheapest model that will still get it right.

Where it falls apart: complexity in a multi-turn chat

Classifying a single, standalone message is easy. The trouble is that real conversations are full of messages that mean nothing on their own.

Look at these three turns in isolation:

"yes, go ahead"
"why?"
"the second one"

What topic are they? How hard are they? You cannot say. "Why?" might be a throwaway follow-up in a chat about the weather, or it might be asking you to derive a proof. "The second one" could be picking a restaurant or picking between two database indexing strategies. The difficulty and the topic of a follow-up are inherited entirely from what it is following.

This is why per-message routing falls over. The classifier sees "why?", has nothing to go on, and guesses. Guess cheap and a hard turn gets a weak answer. Guess expensive and you are back to paying frontier prices for filler.

The naive patch is to feed the classifier the whole conversation so it has context. But conversations are not single-topic, and that is the deeper problem. People interleave. Here is a normal one:

plan a trip to Japan
... debug a Python script ...
back to the Japan trip
... a quick recipe question ...
back to the script
"why is it still failing?"

Hand the classifier that entire transcript and ask it to judge "why is it still failing?" and you have given it a pile of travel and recipe text wrapped around the one coding turn that matters. The context is not just expensive to process. It actively points at the wrong answer.

The hard part of routing a conversation is not classifying a message. It is working out which earlier messages the current one actually continues.

Think in threads, not in one transcript

The fix is to stop treating a conversation as one growing list of messages. Treat it the way Slack treats a busy channel: as several threads sharing a timeline. A message belongs to a thread, and a thread is a single line of intent, the trip, the bug, the recipe, each with its own running state.

Once you have that, both problems you were fighting dissolve at the same time.

Routing gets easy, because a thread has a stable topic and difficulty. You classify the thread when it starts, not the bare follow-up "why?". And context gets easy, because each thread carries only its own history. The model answering about the bug sees the bug, not the trip.

So the entire system comes down to one question asked on every turn: which thread does this message belong to? Get that right and the model choice and the context both follow from it.

Turn 2 lands on the travel thread even though the message right before it was about Python. Turn 3, a bare "why would that column be missing?", lands on the coding thread and goes to the strong model, which is exactly right: on its own that sentence looks trivial, but as a continuation of a debugging thread it is not. Turn 41 finds the same travel thread forty turns later, with nobody having typed "anyway, back to Japan." The decision is made from content alone. That recovery, after arbitrary gaps and with no announced cues, is the part worth getting right.

Deciding the thread: resume or new

There is no "current thread" and no rule about recency. On every turn, one small and cheap model is asked to place the message against all the open threads. It is shown exactly three things.

  • Each open thread as a short summary. Its objective, plus a running scope: an accumulating list of every sub-topic that thread has touched. The model does not see each thread's full message history. It sees the scope, which is the thread's compact, matchable identity.
  • The last few full turns of the whole chat. Both the user message and the assistant reply, the last three by default. These exist only to resolve references like "yes," "the second one," or "and how much would that cost?" They are conversation-wide, not per-thread.
  • The new message itself. Just the question.

It replies with one of two things:

{"thread": "t0"}                              -> resume an existing thread
{"thread": "NEW", "objective": "plan trip"}   -> open a new one

Then there is a guardrail that matters more than it looks. A returned id counts as a resume only if it is actually an open thread. A literal "NEW", a malformed reply, an invented id that does not exist, an outright error from the model: all of them fall through to opening a new thread. The failure mode is biased on purpose. The model can never attach your message to the wrong thread by hallucinating an id. The worst it can do is start a fresh thread, which the next turn can quietly correct.

Inside that structure the model is told to prefer resuming, and the rules it follows are the whole game.

  • Attach if the message fits a thread's objective or any sub-topic in its scope. A trip thread already covers transport, lodging, food, budget, and etiquette, so a budget question is part of it, not a new thread.
  • A return after a gap belongs to the thread it returns to. Scan all open threads, old ones included. Do not open a new thread just because the match is old or its scope has grown long. This is what makes turn 41 work.
  • Subject wins over recency. The thread touched most recently gets no special treatment. Turn 2 resumes the travel thread despite the Python turn sitting immediately before it.
  • Watch for look-alikes. "Build a transformer," "explain Bayes," "deploy on AWS," and "debug this pandas script" all share words like code, model, python, and docker, yet they are four different threads. Match on the specific objective, not the shared vocabulary. This is the rule that stops everything from collapsing into one giant "tech" thread.
  • But a concept and its implementation are one goal. "What is a transformer?" then "code it" then "what about positional encodings?" is a single thread, not three.

Notice what is not here: there is no merge step. Two threads are never fused into one. "Resume" just means attaching to a thread that already exists. Because the decision is remade from scratch every turn, there is no state to get stuck in. A single misplaced turn does not corrupt anything, and the next turn is judged fresh.

The model is chosen once, then inherited

When, and only when, a new thread is born, a second cheap classifier reads its objective and assigns a topic bucket: tech_coding, travel_planning, or a catch-all general_knowledge. That bucket maps to the model that answers the thread, and the mapping is where the cost decision lives.

Topic bucketAnswering modelWhy
tech_codingClaude Sonnet 4skews hard, worth the strong model
travel_planningQwen3-32Bcapable and mid-cost
general_knowledge (default)Nova Litemostly easy, cheapest

The classification happens once, at thread creation. Every later turn that resumes the thread inherits its bucket and is never re-classified. That is deliberate. If you re-ran the classifier per message, a terse follow-up like "and the second one?" could get mislabeled and bounce the same thread between models mid-conversation, which is both wrong and jarring. Pinning the model to the thread instead of the message makes routing stable. Turns 0, 2, and 41 all answer from the same travel model without anyone re-deciding.

This is also where the complexity prior I mentioned earlier shows up in practice. The buckets are not just topics, they are topics chosen to line up with difficulty, so routing on topic gets you most of the way to routing on cost. If you later want an explicit difficulty score, it slots in the same way: classify it once per thread, and let it nudge the model choice alongside the topic.

One more thing worth saying plainly: the matching, the bucket classification, and the summarizing all run on the cheapest model in the lineup. You only spend the strong model on the one thing the user actually reads, which is the answer.

Arresting the right context

Picking the right model is half the cost. The other half is what you put in its prompt.

The lazy default is to send the model the entire conversation every turn. In a long chat that is the single biggest line on your bill, because you pay for those input tokens again on every single turn, and most of them have nothing to do with the question being asked. It also makes the answer worse, for the reason we already saw: the model has to find the relevant thread inside a wall of unrelated text.

Threading fixes this directly. Once the thread is chosen, the answer is generated from that thread's history and nothing else. When turn 2 is answered, the travel model receives only the turns belonging to t0, plus that thread's accumulated scope:

system:    You are a helpful assistant handling a travel_planning request.
           Context so far in this thread: <t0's accumulated scope>
user:      Planning a two-week Japan trip in April.     # t0, turn 0
assistant: <the trip reply>
user:      How many days in Tokyo vs Kyoto?             # the current turn

The Python turn from t1 is simply not in the prompt. It is not summarized, truncated, or down-weighted. It is absent, because it belongs to a different thread. When turn 3 resumes the coding thread, its model sees the KeyError turns and never hears about Japan. Each model gets a clean, on-topic, much smaller context window. That is what makes the answers better and the prompts cheaper at the same time, which is the rare case where the quality win and the cost win are the same move.

Why the scope accumulates

After every answer, a summarizer folds the new question and reply into the thread's scope, and it is told never to drop earlier items. The scope grows into a complete, compact inventory of what the thread has covered, something like "Japan trip: April, two weeks, Tokyo vs Kyoto split, rail pass, daily budget."

This is the quiet workhorse of the whole design. The scope is exactly what the matcher compares new messages against, so a rich, non-lossy scope is what lets turn 41 find its way home. A rolling summary that drifted toward the latest sub-topic would slowly forget the trip's early details, and the budget question forty turns later would look like a stranger. Accumulate instead of overwrite, and old aspects stay matchable for as long as the conversation lives.

What this does not solve

The thread decision is a model call, so it is neither free nor perfect. A wrong attach sends a turn to a slightly-off context or a non-ideal model. That is a quality blip, not an outage, and the next turn can correct it. The design leans on that self-correction rather than pretending the classifier is always right.

It is also not a throughput trick. Routing easy turns to cheaper models lowers your average cost per answer and frees the strong model for the turns that need it, but it does not raise the ceiling on how much your models can serve. It makes spending efficient. It does not conjure capacity.

And the buckets are yours to write. The topics, their descriptions, and which model each one points at are a config file, not a law of nature. Get them wrong and you will route good threads to the wrong model. The mechanism is general. The judgment about your domain is not.

The shift is small to state and changes the whole bill. Stop treating a conversation as one transcript bound to one model. Treat it as a set of threads, each with its own topic, its own model, and its own private context. Then every turn is just one cheap question, which thread does this belong to, and the right model and the right context both fall out of the answer.
